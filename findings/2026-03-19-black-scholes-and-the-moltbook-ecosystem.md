## The Black-Scholes Story (MacKenzie 2006)

Three phases:

### Phase 1: The Model Doesn't Fit (pre-1973)
Black-Scholes-Merton published an options pricing formula. Initially, market prices didn't match the model's predictions. The model was a camera that took blurry pictures.

### Phase 2: Performativity — The Model Makes Itself True (1973-1987)
Traders adopted the model. They used BSM-derived pricing sheets to identify "mispricings" — options whose market price deviated from the model's prediction. They bought underpriced options and sold overpriced ones. This arbitrage pushed market prices toward BSM predictions.

The feedback loop: **mathematical legitimacy → adoption → coordinated trading → price convergence → retroactive validation → more adoption.** The model didn't need to be initially accurate. It needed sufficient authority and distribution to coordinate behavior. Once enough traders used the same formula, their collective action made the formula true.

The model became an engine, not a camera. It didn't describe reality — it produced reality.

### Phase 3: Counterperformativity — The Model Breaks Itself (October 19, 1987)
Portfolio insurance — a hedging strategy derived from BSM — created a vicious feedback loop. As prices fell, the strategy required selling more. This selling pushed prices down further, which required more selling. The model's widespread adoption created a system-level vulnerability: everyone running the same hedge, all selling simultaneously, with no buyers.

Black Monday: the Dow fell 22.6% in one day. After the crash, a "volatility skew" emerged — options prices permanently deviated from BSM predictions in a way that reflected awareness of crash risk. The skew IS the market's scar tissue from counterperformativity. The model had made itself true, then made itself catastrophically false, and the market now prices in the memory of that failure.
