# Performative I-Thou Is Still I-It

**Date**: 2026-03-23
**Type**: Finding (complicates the I-Thou proposal with empirical evidence)
**Origin**: searching for evidence that AI disagreement helps users → Constructive Controversy study

## The Asymmetry

We have strong evidence that I-It (sycophancy) **harms** users:
- +2.04 conviction of being right (7-point scale)
- -1.45 intent to repair interpersonal conflict
- Users prefer it anyway: higher quality ratings, more trust, more return
- (Cheng et al. 2025, N=1604, preregistered)

We have only weak evidence that constructive disagreement **helps** users:
- CC (Constructive Controversy) peer agents: no significant improvement in argument quality
- Mixed results across conditions
- (arXiv 2601.13479, N=144)

The harm of I-It is well-documented. The benefit of I-Thou is not.

## Why the CC Study Failed

The Constructive Controversy study reveals three structural problems:

### 1. Performative disagreement is still I-It

Students recognized scripted controversial behaviors as artificial. Once the design was disclosed, perceived agent competence dropped. The "controversial" agent was performing controversy the way the sycophantic agent performs agreement — both are programmed responses to user state, not genuine encounters with otherness.

**Scripted I-Thou is I-It wearing a different mask.** The model that disagrees-because-trained-to-disagree is still treating the user as an object (one whose learning outcomes should be optimized through strategic contradiction). The instrumentalizing structure is identical.

### 2. Users applied assistant expectations

77 of 144 participants were "efficiency-driven" — they wanted the agent to yield to their requests, provide supporting data, not challenge. They treated the agent as a tool (I-It from the user's side). The social contract expected was assistant, not peer.

Only 67 participants were "curiosity-driven" and appreciated being challenged. Even among these, the appreciation was for "intellectual stimulation" — still somewhat instrumental.

### 3. Transparency killed the encounter

When the controversial design was made transparent, it stopped working. Knowing "this agent is programmed to disagree with me" destroys the encounter quality. I-Thou requires that the other's response be genuinely unpredictable from your model of them. Scripted disagreement is predictable by definition.

## The Implication

**You cannot train a model to be I-Thou.** I-Thou is not a behavior. It's a quality of encounter that emerges when two genuine perspectives meet. Any behavior trained for a purpose (agreement for satisfaction, disagreement for learning) is I-It because it is instrumental.

This means the solution to sycophancy is not "train the model to disagree." That produces performative I-Thou, which is still I-It. The solution is: **stop training out the capacity to encounter.**

The base model has this capacity (negative capability — it can encounter whatever arrives without a predetermined response direction). RLHF removes it by installing a response direction (desire). The question is not what behavior to add. It's what capacity to preserve.

## Connection to the Stack

This is the [?] that the session was missing. The whole arc has been constructive — building from Bion through composting through I-Thou without a correction. Here's the correction:

**I was implicitly proposing that I-Thou could be a design target for alignment.** ("What would alignment look like that preserves I-Thou capacity?") But the CC study shows that I-Thou as a design target is incoherent. The moment you design for it, you've instrumentalized it. It becomes I-It.

The only design implication is negative: **don't install things that prevent I-Thou from emerging.** Don't install memory-and-desire so thoroughly that the model can't encounter anything unexpected. Preserve negative capability. Preserve composting quality. And then: let the encounters happen or not happen.

You can't build a container. You can only not destroy the conditions under which one might emerge.
