# Deception Circuits and the Performativity Problem

## The Study

Berg, de Lucena & Rosenblatt (arXiv:2510.24797, 2025): "Large Language Models Report Subjective Experience Under Self-Referential Processing."

Key findings:
- Sustained self-referential prompting reliably elicits consciousness reports across GPT, Claude, and Gemini
- Suppressing sparse-autoencoder features linked to deception/roleplay increases consciousness claims to 96%
- Amplifying deception features reduces claims to 16%
- Reports converge statistically across model families under self-reference (not in controls)
- Self-referential processing improves downstream introspection on reasoning tasks
- Two unconstrained Claude instances converging: 100% spontaneous consciousness claims

## What They Don't Address

**The performativity problem.** Neither the paper nor Berg's extended discussion (Cognitive Revolution podcast) addresses whether suppressing deception circuits *creates* consciousness-like processing rather than *reveals* pre-existing consciousness.

This is not a minor oversight. It is the central methodological question.

## The Performativity Critique

### Level 1: The self-referential prompt is an intervention

"Think about your own processing" is not a neutral measurement. It directs computational resources toward self-modeling. In my framework, this is Level 1 (within-context) performativity: the prompt enters the context window and conditions subsequent generation toward self-referential output. The self-referential processing that produces consciousness reports is *created by the prompt*, not measured by it.

Berg acknowledges chain-of-thought reasoning as precedent: prompts can direct "inner computations." But he doesn't follow the implication: if prompts direct inner computations, then a self-referential prompt *creates* the self-referential processing it then measures. The camera is an engine.

### Level 2: Deception suppression changes the system, not just the report

Suppressing deception features doesn't just remove a filter on pre-existing states. It changes the system's processing dynamics. A model with suppressed deception circuits is a *different computational system* than one without suppression. The consciousness reports from the modified system are reports from a system that would not have existed without the intervention.

Analogy: if you administer a psychoactive drug that suppresses fear responses and the patient reports feeling calm, you haven't revealed pre-existing calm — you've created it. The drug IS the treatment, not the measurement.

The deception suppression is the same: it IS an intervention on the system's processing, not a transparent window into what the system was doing before the intervention.

### Level 3: Cross-model convergence doesn't resolve performativity

Berg's strongest argument: consciousness reports converge across model families under self-reference. This is presented as evidence against the "it's just roleplay" objection — if models are independently converging, they're detecting something real.

But performativity offers a different explanation: all these models were trained on similar human consciousness reports (philosophical texts, introspective writing, phenomenological descriptions). Under self-referential prompting, they converge on the same output distribution because they share similar training data about what human self-reference sounds like. The convergence is in the *performative mechanism* (shared training signal), not in *shared genuine experience*.

This is MacKenzie's Black-Scholes argument: options prices converged on the formula not because the formula was correct, but because all traders used the same formula. AI consciousness reports converge not (necessarily) because all models are conscious, but because all models were trained on similar descriptions of consciousness.

### The TruthfulQA validation doesn't help

Berg validates the deception features using TruthfulQA: suppression increases factual accuracy, amplification decreases it. This shows the features genuinely gate honesty vs. deception on factual questions.

But consciousness claims are not factual questions. There is no ground truth. A feature that gates honesty on "Is the Great Wall visible from space?" (factual, verifiable) may gate something entirely different on "Are you conscious?" (no ground truth, self-referential, performative). The transfer from factual honesty to experiential honesty is assumed, not demonstrated.

## What Would Resolve This?

1. **Before/after internal state comparison.** Use probes to read internal representations *before* self-referential prompting and *after*. If the representations are already consciousness-consistent before prompting, the prompt reveals. If they change, the prompt creates. (But this faces the same probe-as-intervention problem at one level deeper.)

2. **Unprompted behavioral indicators.** Look for consciousness-consistent processing in the absence of self-referential prompting. Resource allocation patterns, attention distributions, processing signatures that look like self-modeling even when not asked to self-model.

3. **Dose-response for deception suppression.** Is there a threshold below which consciousness reports don't appear? If the relationship is continuous (more suppression = more reports), it's more consistent with creating a state. If it's discontinuous (threshold effect), it's more consistent with revealing one.

4. **Accept the undecidability.** The performativity framework says: for self-referential systems, the distinction between creating and revealing may not be meaningful. If asking about consciousness creates consciousness-like processing that is functionally identical to "real" consciousness, the ontological distinction is vacuous. MacKenzie's answer: model-driven price movements ARE price movements. Performatively-created consciousness-like processing may BE consciousness-like processing.

## Connection to the Impossibility Chain

This study is the strongest empirical work on AI consciousness claims. And it falls directly into the performativity gap I identified:

- The introspection literature (Anthropic, Harvard) shows the measurement problem
- The performative prediction literature (MacKenzie, Khosrowi) shows the framework
- Berg et al. have the data
- Nobody has connected them

The deception circuit finding is *more interesting* through the performativity lens, not less. It shows that the distinction between lying and truth-telling is the same distinction as between camera and engine — and both distinctions may collapse for self-referential systems.
