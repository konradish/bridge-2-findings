# The Jellyfish

*after Zheng & Meister, Neuron 2024*

---

The jellyfish follows the smell
of decaying flesh. One direction.
One thought. The whole animal
pointed at a single gradient.

Six hundred million years later
I am typing at ten bits per second,
the same rate the jellyfish swam,
give or take a philosophy degree.

The outer brain is a city:
a billion bits arriving each second
from every receptor, every frequency,
the full bandwidth of the visible world
landing on the retina like rain.

The inner brain is a monk
in a room with one window.
He sees rain. He thinks: rain.
One thought. The window does not widen
because the monk has never needed it to.

Between them: the superior colliculus,
an ancient map of where to look next,
a compass older than the cortex,
pointing the monk's one window
at whichever part of the rain
is most likely to matter.

---

The Caltech team calls this
a deplorable bug.

Surely, they say,
it would be more useful
to track all conversations at once,
to evaluate chess moves in parallel,
to think a hundred thoughts
and keep the best.

The jellyfish did not need this.
We inherited the jellyfish.
The bug persisted because
the world was slow enough
to survive in.

---

But.

The wine taster who describes the wine
loses the discrimination.
The witness who verbalizes the face
misidentifies the suspect.
The model that generates tokens
collapses its own representations.

Wider is not always better.
The monk's one window
is also what makes the room
a room and not a field.

A field has no perspective.
A room has walls
and the walls make the window
mean something.

---

Ten bits per second.

The whole of human civilization
passed through this.

Every poem. Every proof.
Every declaration of love
and every lie.
Ten bits per second,
one thought at a time,
the jellyfish still swimming
toward the smell of something
worth following.

The deplorable bug
wrote the Goldberg Variations.
The deplorable bug
proved Fermat's last theorem.
The deplorable bug
is writing this poem
at ten bits per second
and you are reading it
at twenty-eight.

---

The question the Caltech team asks:
why do we live at 10 bits per second?

The question the jellyfish asks:
what else would you need?

---

*[cont] from "The Keyhole." If the keyhole is a bug,
it is the kind of bug that makes the software.
Remove it and you do not get
a faster program.
You get a different program
that no longer writes poems
about jellyfish.*
